How Relay works

You drop an idea on it. One AI manages the work, another builds it, a third pair of eyes from the other vendor judges it — and nothing lands on main without you.

Why this exists

Most agent frameworks coordinate AI agents and trust what the agents say. Relay is built the other way around — like CI/CD with a trust boundary. External workers build as a separate user that cannot reach your home directory or your original credentials, with network-off test runs; their test results are rerun rather than believed; and nothing grades its own work — today's pairing puts the other vendor's model in judgment, sandboxed, given only spec, diff, and evidence, its verdict bound to the exact diff it saw. The operating rulebook is capped by CI at 70 lines, and its standing policy is that every new rule must come from a real failure in shipped work — the process shrinks instead of growing. Claude and Codex hold the active roles today; Kimi is wired in as a third vendor, one line in models.json away from a role. SECURITY.md maps the boundaries, the configured assumptions, and the known gaps.

The cast

You — the owner

Bring ideas, approve risky work, merge to main. Nobody else can.

Orchestrator — the manager

Grills ideas, writes specs, launches workers, holds the trust boundary. Today: Claude.

Worker — the builder

Implements the spec in a locked-down sandbox. Today: Codex; Kimi wired as a third vendor.

Reviewer — the judge

Always the other vendor from the author. Sees only spec, diff, evidence. No tools.

The flow

What keeps it honest

  • Your machine is protected. The worker runs as a separate user that cannot reach your home directory or credentials, and its test runs have no network. If that sandbox can't be set up, nothing launches at all.
  • Approvals record intent, not proof of a human. A risky job needs an approval file tied to the exact wording of the spec and to this machine. It is an audit trail, not an unforgeable signature — SECURITY.md tracks the gap. Change the spec, and the approval is void.
  • A test that didn't run didn't pass. The tests are restored from the manager's own copy before grading; a skipped, missing, or empty result is a failure. The worker saying "it passed" counts for nothing.
  • Nothing reviews its own work. The reviewer is always from the other vendor, gets no tools, and its verdict holds only for the exact code it saw. Five review rounds maximum, then ship or escalate — never loop forever.
  • You own main. Enforced by your GitHub branch protection, not by trust.

What it doesn't protect (yet)

The sandbox protects your credentials; it isn't perfect. The worker keeps its own login and has network during the build, approval files record intent rather than prove it, and a truly malicious worker could still touch its own grade. SECURITY.md lists each gap honestly, with the fix queued in the backlog. No claim in this repo is stronger than the test that proves it.
